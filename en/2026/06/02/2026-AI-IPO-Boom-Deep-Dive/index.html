⚠ Hidden Financial Engineering: Cloud Credits Circular Financing

In this AI upstart IPO feast, a financial engineering scheme known as “ Cloud Credits Circular Financing” is systematically distorting supply and demand signals in capital markets.
Tech giants (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Nvidia) inject funds into AI startups under the guise of “ investment,” but the funds are not cash — they are issued in the form of monopolistic cloud service credits (Cloud Credits).
These startups are forced to use the credits to purchase computing power from their investors, and the funds flow back to the giants’accounts in the form of “ server purchases/chip procurement,” and are recorded as the giants’ “ cloud business revenue”.
The same money is counted twice — once as investment, once as revenue — thereby manufacturing false demand signals and valuation bubbles.

Nasdaq’s 5x Multiplier Rule: The “ Nuclear Weapon” for Low Free Float Stocks

Nasdaq introduced a highly controversial “5x multiplier rule” in its March 2026 rule modification:
for mega-IPOs with free float ratios below 10%, when calculating index weight, their market capitalization is calculated at 5 times the actual free float.

What does this mean, Suppose SpaceX goes public at a $1.75 trillion valuation but only releases 5% free float (approximately $87.5 billion),
under the 5x multiplier rule, its weight in the Nasdaq 100 index would be calculated based on $437.5 billion — which equals 5 times its actual free float and 25% of its total valuation.

The direct consequence of this rule is that passive funds are forced to allocate these low-free-float stocks at extremely high weights,
while early insiders gain enormous index weight premiums on a minimal free float base.
This is a brazen “ leveraging small to control large” arbitrage mechanism.

Potential Consequences of Unregulated Loss of Control

Index providers are private companies whose rule modifications are not directly regulated by the SEC. This regulatory vacuum could lead to the following consequences:

  • Rule Arbitrariness: Index providers can modify rules at any time based on specific clients’needs without explanation or hearing.

  • Conflicts of Interest: Index providers may receive benefits from IPO underwriters, thereby tending to modify rules to favor specific IPOs.

  • Systemic Risk: When trillions in passive funds are forced to allocate according to distorted rules, market pricing mechanisms will be severely distorted.

  • Retail Investor Harm: Ultimately bearing the costs are ordinary workers who indirectly invest in indices through 401(k) s and pension funds.

Echoes of History: The Mirror Image of the AI Bubble and the Dot-Com Bubble

The AI IPO boom led by SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic in 2026 forms a striking historical mirror image with the dot-com bubble twenty-five years ago.
Both bubbles carried grand narratives of technological revolution, both experienced frenzied capital inflows, and both face the ultimate test of productivity realization.
However, the true value of history lies not in simple analogy, but in understanding the subtle and dangerous dividing line between technological revolution and capital bubbles.

Productivity Leap: The True Gift of Technological Revolution

History tells us that the bursting of technology bubbles often leaves genuine legacies. After the 2000 dot-com bubble burst,
the overbuilding of fiber infrastructure laid the foundation for subsequent broadband proliferation, and Amazon and Google rose from the ruins to become trillion-dollar giants.

The AI technology revolution similarly possesses genuine potential. Data from the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis shows that AI investment has contributed 39% of GDP growth.
In areas such as medical diagnosis, drug discovery, code generation, and scientific computing, AI is indeed producing quantifiable efficiency improvements.

The question is —are these genuine productivity improvements sufficient to sustain the current $3 trillion valuation?
The capital output ratio imbalance of 42:1 means that behind every dollar of AI revenue, there is $42 in computing investment.
This imbalance cannot persist indefinitely.

The Heavy Lesson of 2000: Bursting Is Not a Gentle Correction

The NASDAQ Composite Index reached its peak of 5,048 in March 2000, then experienced a 31-month decline,
hitting bottom at 1,114 in October 2002 —a decline of 78%.
This means investors who bought at the peak lost more than three-quarters of their wealth.

An even more far-reaching impact was the innovation winter. From 2000 to 2005, Silicon Valley venture capital plummeted 80%,
countless startups folded, and tech talent dispersed. It wasn’t until the iPhone launch in 2007 that the tech industry truly emerged from the trough.

If the 2026 AI bubble follows a similar script, the consequences could be even more severe — because this bubble’s participants include not only risk-tolerant active investors,
but also hundreds of millions of ordinary workers who are forced to participate through passive index funds.

The Most Dangerous Harvest: “ Guaranteed Absorption” Under the Passive Index Mechanism

In this bubble, the most outrageous aspect is not the inflated valuations, but the institutionalized wealth transfer mechanism.
Nasdaq and S&P 500 successively modified index inclusion rules in 2026, shortening the observation period for mega unprofitable giants from years to 15 trading days,
and removing the consecutive profitability threshold. This is not a neutral “ market rule adjustment,” but an exit channel tailor-made for specific interest groups.

When SpaceX and Anthropic are forcibly included in key benchmark indices such as the S&P 100 and Nasdaq 100 just 15 days after listing,
trillions of dollars in global retirement funds and index ETFs must unconditionally buy — regardless of whether their valuations are reasonable,
whether their business models are sustainable, whether retail investors wish to participate. The essence of this “ passive absorption” mechanism is
to transfer the exit risk of early VCs, founders, and insiders through the “ mechanical nature” of index investing,
forcibly imposing it on every ordinary worker contributing to a pension fund.

This is the most sophisticated and most ruthless wealth extraction in modern financial history: equity acquired by early capital at pennies
is sold to the public market at valuations of hundreds of dollars per share at IPO; and when stock prices ultimately revert to fundamentals,
those bearing the losses are the index fund holders who never actively chose to invest in these companies — teachers, nurses, firefighters, factory workers’retirement savings. These capitalists who harvest retail investors by modifying rules and exploiting “ guaranteed absorption” by passive funds
are not value-creating investors, but wealth-transferring predators.

Final Judgment: Between Hope and Warning

The genuine potential of the AI technology revolution is undeniable, but technological revolution and capital bubbles are never synonymous.
The legacy of the dot-com bubble was Amazon and Google, but its cost was the wealth destruction of a generation of investors and a five-year innovation winter.
If the 2026 AI bubble bursts, its legacy may be computing infrastructure and breakthroughs in certain vertical domains,
but its cost may be systemic shock to the global financial system and pension shrinkage for tens of millions of ordinary workers.

Even more dangerous, this bubble’s harvesting mechanism is far more concealed and institutionalized than that of 2000.
When passive index funds become tools for “ guaranteed absorption,” when index rule modifications become channels for benefit transfers,
the “ price discovery” function of market economies has been severely distorted. This is not a failure of free markets, but the conscious manipulation of rules.

Core Judgment Framework

Key Variables

**History does not repeat itself, but it rhymes. The 2000 dot-com bubble taught us: technological revolutions will ultimately be realized, but the vast majority of bubble participants will become fuel rather than passengers. If the 2026 AI bubble follows the same script, the cruelest difference is —this time, retail investors don’t even have the right to choose not to participate, their pension funds have been tied to this gamble by the modifiers of index rules. For professional investors and regulators, while pursuing the trillion-dollar tech mythology, they must maintain the highest level of vigilance against the predatory nature of the passive absorption mechanism, the financial black hole of high computing depreciation, and the systemic risk of bank credit. Technology is worth believing in, but capitalists’greed is always worth questioning.
