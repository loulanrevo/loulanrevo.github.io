Part 01 — Context

What Happened

The 2026 global IPO market exhibits a counterintuitive structural feature: while overall deal count declined 15% year-over-year, total proceeds surged 45% year-over-year, reaching $42.6 billion in Q1—the highest level since 2022. This extreme capital concentration peaked in the second half of 2026 at an unprecedented scale.

The "AI Big Three"—SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic—have a combined valuation approaching $3 trillion. More importantly, their planned listing windows overlap tightly—SpaceX is expected to debut as early as mid-June, Anthropic is targeting the fall, and OpenAI is aiming for year-end or a slip into early 2027—creating enormous liquidity absorption pressure on global public markets.

Using the standard public-market assumption of a 5% free float, these three companies would collectively raise nearly $200 billion in the public markets, exceeding the total proceeds of all U.S. IPOs with market caps above $50 million from 2022 through Q1 2026. That figure alone illustrates the historic scale of this IPO wave.

The most striking story in this IPO race is Anthropic's "come-from-behind surge." Anthropic's ability to overtake OpenAI lies in its diametrically opposite strategic positioning: while OpenAI relies heavily on subscription users (ChatGPT Plus) and API calls in a "consumer-first" strategy, Anthropic has invested 80% of its effort from the start in enterprise (B2B) customer development, with only 20% of revenue coming from consumers. This yields extremely high predictability and consistency, satisfying the rigid compliance and auditability demands of multinational banks, top law firms, and healthcare groups. This has built an unshakeable moat in highly regulated scenarios such as finance, healthcare, and defense.

⚠️ Hidden Financial Engineering: Cloud Credits Circular Financing

In this AI upstart IPO feast, a financial engineering technique called "Cloud Credits Circular Financing" is systematically distorting supply-and-demand signals in the capital markets. Tech giants (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Nvidia) inject capital into AI startups in the name of "investment," but the capital is not cash—it is issued as monopolistic cloud-service credits (Cloud Credits). These startups are forced to use the credits to buy compute from the investor, and the money flows back to the giant in the form of "server/chip purchases," recorded as the giant's "cloud business revenue." The same dollar is counted twice—once as investment, once as revenue—manufacturing false demand signals and valuation bubbles.

Paper Profit vs. Real Cash Flow: A Shocking Contrast

The toxic fruit of this circular financing is directly visible in the giants' financial statements. Alphabet reported a record $62.6 billion profit in Q1 2026, but $28.7 billion of it (nearly half) came from paper gains on its own mark-to-market Anthropic equity holdings—not from actual revenue in Search, YouTube, or Cloud. In the same quarter, Amazon reported $30.3 billion in profit, of which $16.8 billion similarly came from unrealized valuation gains on Anthropic equity. Yet Amazon's free cash flow plummeted 95% to just $1.2 billion—because the company spent $44.2 billion building physical data centers.

The broader systemic risk is that OpenAI and Anthropic together account for more than half of the $2 trillion in future cloud backlog orders at Microsoft, Oracle, Google, and Amazon. Microsoft's $13 billion OpenAI investment was issued as Azure credits, and OpenAI's annual cloud spend ($60 billion) is already more than twice its own revenue ($25 billion); 54% of Oracle's $553 billion pipeline depends on the single customer OpenAI. These "paper profits" contrast starkly with collapsing free cash flow, and analysts have compared this pattern to Global Crossing and Qwest Communications during the 2001 dot-com bubble—when the two companies swapped fiber capacity to manufacture fake sales, ultimately leading Qwest to write off $1.4 billion in revenue and Global Crossing to file for bankruptcy.

Part 06 — The Mechanism

Passive Backstop Mechanism: The Hidden Channel of Index Arbitrage

In this IPO boom, there is a widely overlooked but crucial structural factor: the modern passive index investing system provides these AI giants with an almost deterministic liquidity channel.

According to internal details disclosed by the Financial Times, to accommodate the concentrated arrival of trillion-dollar IPOs such as SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic, Nasdaq (announced March 30, 2026) and S&P 500 (launched consultation April 30, 2026) successively revised their index inclusion criteria, drastically shortening the observation period for mega-cap unprofitable giants and removing the hard threshold of continuous profitability. This means that SpaceX (ticker SPCX) and future Anthropic, after listing for just 15 trading days, will be forced into key benchmarks such as the S&P 100 and Nasdaq-100.

Because retirement funds, pension funds, and index ETFs (such as SPY, QQQ) worth trillions of dollars worldwide must unconditionally buy and hold index constituents— this mechanism essentially ensures that regardless of how severe these AI giants' fundamental losses are, after listing there will be an extremely large pool of passive public capital entering to "backstop" them, providing a deterministic liquidity channel for early high-risk venture investors.

Regulatory Vacuum: An Unwatched Casino

Index providers (S&P Dow Jones, Nasdaq, MSCI, FTSE Russell) are private companies, and their index methodologies are not directly regulated by the SEC. This means:

1. Rule changes require no regulatory approval—index providers can decide when and how to modify inclusion rules without review or approval from any government agency.

2. Conflicts of interest go unsupervised—Nasdaq is both an exchange and an index provider, with a strong incentive to modify rules to attract large IPOs to its platform.

3. SEC stance: encourage rather than regulate—SEC Chairman Paul Atkins has explicitly said he wants to "make IPOs great again," and deregulation is the main theme, not protecting passive investors.

4. Index committee black-box operations—actual S&P 500 inclusion decisions are made by the index committee at its "sole discretion," with non-transparent processes and non-public criteria.

Part 09 — Conclusion

Echoes of History: The Mirror of the AI Bubble and the Internet Bubble

The 2026 AI IPO boom led by SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic forms a stunning historical mirror image with the internet bubble of twenty-five years ago. Both bubbles carried grand narratives of technological revolution, both experienced capital frenzy, and both face the ultimate test of productivity delivery. Yet the true value of history lies not in simple analogy, but in understanding the subtle and dangerous dividing line between technological revolution and capital bubble.

Productivity Leap: The Real Gift of the Technological Revolution

After the internet bubble burst, the market left behind physical infrastructure worth trillions of dollars — transoceanic fiber, data centers, broadband networks. This "excess" capacity became the physical cornerstone of the digital economy for the next two decades. Amazon's stock price plunged 90% after the bubble burst, yet it used cheaply acquired server infrastructure to build the world's largest cloud computing empire; Google reshaped information retrieval and ad distribution relying on widespread broadband networks. Only after the cruel screening of the bubble could the real productivity revolution unfold.

If the AI bubble follows a similar path, its potential legacy may be even more profound. Generative AI has already demonstrated speed advantages surpassing human experts in code generation, drug discovery, materials science, and climate simulation. Even if 95% of enterprise AI projects currently generate zero returns, the remaining 5% — such as Anthropic's Project Glasswing in sovereign cybersecurity and SpaceX applying AI to spacecraft autonomous navigation — may be gestating the core productivity tools of the next era. The key difference: internet infrastructure was "general-purpose," while AI compute investment is highly specialized; once the technical route is wrong, hundreds of billions of dollars of GPU clusters will rapidly become electronic waste.

The Heavy Lesson of 2000: The Burst Is Not a Gentle Correction

The NASDAQ Composite Index crashed from 5,048 in March 2000 to 1,114 in October 2002, a drop of 78%. This is not a "correction," but a devastating wealth wipeout for a generation. Trillions of dollars of market cap evaporated, with the vast majority borne by retail investors — attracted into the market at the bubble peak by the "new economy" narrative, and forced to cut losses at the desperate bottom. The deeper impact is that an entire generation of engineers and entrepreneurs lost confidence in technological innovation, the venture capital industry fell into a five-year deep freeze, and numerous early-stage projects that could have changed the world died due to broken capital chains.

The risks in 2026 may be more hidden and systemic. BofA's AI credit commitments of up to $450 billion account for 25% of its Tier 1 capital, meaning a bubble burst would directly threaten banking system stability. Unlike 2000, today's tech stock concentration has reached an historical extreme — tech stocks' weight in the S&P 500 has broken through 48%. Once AI giant stock prices crash, passive index funds will be forced to sell other constituents to maintain weight balance, triggering cross-sector chain crashes. Pensions, 401(k) retirement plans, university endowments — these funds that should be the most conservative — are deeply tied to this gamble by the "mechanicity" of index investing.

The Most Dangerous Harvest: "Certain Takeover" Under the Passive Index Mechanism

In this bubble, what is most infuriating is not the overvaluation, but the institutionalized wealth-transfer mechanism. Nasdaq and the S&P 500 successively revised index inclusion rules in 2026, shortening the observation period for mega-cap unprofitable giants from several years to 15 trading days, and eliminating the continuous profitability threshold. This is not a neutral "market-rule adjustment," but an exit channel tailor-made for specific interest groups.

When SpaceX and Anthropic are forcibly included in key benchmarks such as the S&P 100 and Nasdaq-100 just 15 days after listing, trillions of dollars of global retirement funds and index ETFs must buy unconditionally — regardless of whether their valuations are reasonable, regardless of whether their business models are sustainable, regardless of whether retail investors are willing. The essence of this "passive takeover" mechanism is to transfer the exit risk of early VCs, founders, and insiders through the "mechanicity" of index investing, forcibly shifting it onto every ordinary worker who pays into a pension.

This is the most elegant and coldest wealth harvest in modern financial history: equity acquired by early capital for pennies is sold to the public market at hundreds of dollars of valuation at IPO; and when stock prices eventually return to fundamentals, those who bear the losses are index-fund holders who never actively chose to invest in these companies — teachers, nurses, firefighters, factory workers' retirement savings. The capitalists who harvest retail investors through rule changes and the use of passive funds' "certain takeover" are not value-creating investors, but wealth-transferring predators.

Final Judgment: Between Hope and Warning

The real potential of the AI technology revolution cannot be denied, but technological revolution and capital bubble have never been synonymous. The legacy of the internet bubble is Amazon and Google, but its cost is the wealth destruction of a generation of investors and a five-year innovation winter. If the 2026 AI bubble bursts, its legacy may be compute infrastructure and breakthroughs in certain vertical fields, but its cost may be systemic shocks to the global financial system and shrinking pensions for tens of millions of ordinary workers.

More dangerously, this bubble's harvesting mechanism is far more hidden and institutionalized than in 2000. When passive index funds become tools for "certain takeover," and index rule revisions become channels for interest transfer, the market economy's "price discovery" function has been severely distorted. This is not a failure of the free market, but a conscious manipulation of the rules.
